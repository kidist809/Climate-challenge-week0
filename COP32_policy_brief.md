# Climate Vulnerability and Adaptation Priorities for COP32: Evidence from Five East African Nations

**A Policy Brief for the Ethiopian Ministry of Planning and Development**

*Prepared for COP32 — Addis Ababa, 2027*

---

## Executive Summary

Analysis of NASA POWER satellite-derived climate data from 2015–2026 reveals significant and statistically significant temperature divergence across five East African nations (p < 0.001). Sudan emerges as the most climate-vulnerable country, recording a mean temperature of 28.76°C with 224.5 extreme heat days annually and 142.8 consecutive dry days per year—conditions that directly threaten agricultural productivity and water security across the Sahel transition zone. Ethiopia, while demonstrating a cooler mean temperature of 16.07°C, remains highly sensitive to precipitation variability (standard deviation 6.29 mm/day), exposing its predominantly rain-fed agricultural sector to monsoon instability. The data demonstrates that temperature alone does not determine vulnerability; rather, the interaction of extreme heat, precipitation volatility, and agricultural dependence creates differentiated risk profiles requiring targeted adaptation finance. This brief calls for: (1) priority climate finance allocation to Sudan for drought resilience infrastructure; (2) enhanced early warning systems across the Horn of Africa; and (3) regional climate-smart agriculture programs targeting rain-fed systems in Ethiopia and Tanzania.

---

## 1. Climate Signal: What is Changing?

### 1.1 Temperature Trends and Regional Differentiation

The five-country analysis reveals substantial temperature heterogeneity across East Africa. Sudan records the highest mean temperature at 28.76°C (standard deviation 4.68°C), followed by Tanzania at 26.80°C and Nigeria at 26.66°C. Kenya and Ethiopia occupy the cooler spectrum at 20.43°C and 16.07°C respectively. The one-way ANOVA confirms these differences are statistically significant (F-statistic = 18,938.75, p < 0.001), indicating genuine climatic divergence rather than random variation.

The temperature variability data reveals critical patterns. Sudan's high standard deviation (4.68°C) reflects extreme seasonal swings between boreal summer (40°C+) and winter periods, creating chronic thermal stress for both human populations and agricultural systems. In contrast, Kenya's low standard deviation (1.44°C) indicates more stable thermal conditions, while Ethiopia's moderate variability (1.90°C) reflects its highland elevation buffering.

### 1.2 Precipitation Patterns and Variability

Precipitation analysis reveals a distinct vulnerability gradient. Sudan records the lowest mean precipitation at 0.64 mm/day with minimal variability (std 3.06 mm/day)—characteristic of arid Sahelian conditions. Nigeria and Tanzania show the highest mean precipitation at 4.21 mm/day and 3.74 mm/day respectively, but also the greatest variability (standard deviations of 7.27 and 8.00 mm/day), indicating intense wet-dry oscillations that destabilize agricultural planning.

Ethiopia's precipitation profile (mean 3.63 mm/day, std 6.29 mm/day) reflects its dependence on the Ethiopian Highland orographic system and the Kiremt seasonal rains (June–September). The variability observed suggests increasing unpredictability in monsoon onset and duration—a finding consistent with broader East African climate projections.

## 2. Impact Analysis: What It Causes in Real Systems

### 2.1 Agricultural Sector Vulnerabilities

The temperature and precipitation findings translate directly into agricultural risk. Sudan's 224.5 extreme heat days annually (days exceeding 35°C) create near-continuous heat stress during the growing season, particularly damaging to sorghum and millet cultivation—the staple crops supporting 80% of rural livelihoods. The 142.8 average consecutive dry days represent complete crop failure risk for rain-fed agriculture, effectively rendering large portions of Sudan's agricultural zone climatically marginal.

Ethiopia's precipitation variability (std 6.29 mm/day) poses a distinct but equally serious threat. The country's agricultural sector, which employs approximately 80% of the workforce and contributes 45% of GDP, remains predominantly rain-fed. Variability in the Kiremt seasonal rains directly affects cereal production (teff, wheat, maize), with direct implications for national food security and price stability. The 2020–2023 Horn of Africa drought demonstrated this vulnerability, with Ethiopia experiencing significant crop losses and humanitarian emergencies.

Tanzania's high precipitation variability (std 8.00 mm/day) similarly threatens maize and rice production in the southern highlands and coastal zones, where smallholder farmers lack irrigation alternatives. Nigeria's high mean precipitation (4.21 mm/day) masks extreme intra-annual variability that disrupts planting calendars and increases both flood and drought risk.

### 2.2 Water Resources and Hydrological Stress

The consecutive dry days analysis reveals critical water security implications. Sudan's 142.8 average dry days creates chronic groundwater depletion and surface water scarcity, affecting both rural water supply and the Blue Nile system that provides 60% of Egypt's Nile water flow. This transboundary dimension amplifies Sudan's climate vulnerability into a regional security concern.

Kenya and Tanzania's moderate dry day counts (41.1 and 40.2 days respectively) indicate emerging water stress, particularly in urban centers (Nairobi, Dar es Salaam) where population growth outpaces infrastructure development. Ethiopia's 37.9 consecutive dry days, while lower than regional averages, still poses risks to the Awash Basin and other critical irrigated zones.

### 2.3 Livelihoods and Food Security

The convergence of extreme heat, precipitation variability, and agricultural dependence creates compound livelihood risks. In Sudan, the combination of extreme heat and prolonged dry spells has contributed to documented pasture degradation, livestock productivity decline, and rural-urban migration patterns consistent with climate-induced displacement. World Bank climate risk profiles for Sudan document agricultural GDP losses of 2–4% in drought years, with cascading effects on food price inflation and malnutrition rates.

Ethiopia's sensitivity to precipitation variability directly affects the 15+ million people who depend on subsistence agriculture in the highlands. Seasonal rainfall variability translates into harvest uncertainty, with implications for household food security and market price stability across the national economy.

## 3. Comparative Vulnerability Analysis

### 3.1 Sudan: Maximum Climate Stress

Sudan emerges as the most climate-vulnerable nation in the analysis, with vulnerability driven by three interacting factors:

1. **Extreme thermal load**: Mean temperature 28.76°C with 224.5 extreme heat days annually creates chronic heat stress affecting human health, agricultural labor productivity, and ecosystem function.

2. **Arid precipitation regime**: Mean precipitation of only 0.64 mm/day places Sudan in a permanent water deficit state, with minimal buffer against inter-annual variability.

3. **Agricultural dependence**: Over 60% of Sudan's population depends on rain-fed agriculture, directly exposed to the 142.8 consecutive dry days that eliminate growing season viability.

The data does not support simplistic narratives of "cooler is safer." Rather, Sudan's vulnerability stems from the compound interaction of extreme heat, precipitation scarcity, and economic dependence on climate-sensitive agriculture.

### 3.2 Ethiopia: Moderate but Sensitive

Ethiopia's vulnerability profile demonstrates that moderate temperatures do not eliminate climate risk. Three factors create sensitivity:

1. **Precipitation variability**: Standard deviation of 6.29 mm/day indicates significant inter-annual variability in the Kiremt rains, directly affecting agricultural output.

2. **Agricultural dependence**: Over 80% employment in agriculture creates massive exposure to rainfall variability, with limited irrigation alternatives.

3. **Highland ecosystem fragility**: The Ethiopian Highlands, while cooler, face deforestation, soil erosion, and water retention challenges that amplify climate impacts.

Ethiopia's position as host of COP32 creates both opportunity and responsibility. The data supports positioning Ethiopia as a climate-resilient model—not because it faces less climate change, but because its highland elevation and diverse agroecological zones provide natural adaptation capacity that can be enhanced through targeted investment.

## 4. Policy Implications: What Action Is Required

### 4.1 Sudan: Priority Adaptation Finance

The data demands urgent adaptation finance allocation to Sudan, targeting:

**Drought Resilience Infrastructure**: The 142.8 consecutive dry days require investment in small-scale irrigation, water harvesting structures, and groundwater development to provide agricultural alternatives during dry spells.

**Heat-Adaptive Agriculture**: Support for heat-tolerant crop varieties (sorghum, millet), agroforestry systems providing thermal buffering, and shifting cultivation calendars to avoid peak heat periods.

**Early Warning Systems**: Meteorological capacity building to provide seasonal forecasts that enable anticipatory action rather than reactive humanitarian response.

**Estimated financing need**: USD 200–300 million over 5 years for irrigation infrastructure, early warning systems, and climate-smart agriculture programs.

### 4.2 Ethiopia: Rain-Fed System Resilience

Ethiopia requires targeted investment in precipitation-dependent agricultural systems:

**Climate-Smart Agriculture**: Promotion of drought-tolerant crop varieties, conservation agriculture practices, and integrated soil moisture management in the highlands.

**Irrigation Development**: Expansion of small-scale irrigation in the Rift Valley and eastern highlands to reduce dependence on seasonal rainfall.

**Weather Index Insurance**: Scaling of crop insurance products that protect smallholder farmers against rainfall variability, building on existing pilot programs.

**Seasonal Forecast Integration**: Enhanced meteorological services providing actionable seasonal forecasts to agricultural extension services and farmer cooperatives.

**Estimated financing need**: USD 150–250 million over 5 years for irrigation, climate-smart agriculture, and early warning integration.

### 4.3 Regional Cooperation: Horn of Africa Climate Resilience

The cross-border nature of climate impacts requires regional coordination:

**Shared Early Warning Systems**: Development of harmonized regional climate monitoring and seasonal forecast systems covering Ethiopia, Sudan, Kenya, and Tanzania.

**Transboundary Water Management**: Joint investment in Blue Nile basin management (Ethiopia-Sudan) and Lake Victoria basin resilience (Kenya-Tanzania).

**Regional Climate Finance Facility**: Establishment of a Horn of Africa Climate Resilience Fund pooling international adaptation finance for regional distribution based on vulnerability analysis.

**Regional Agricultural Research**: Joint development of heat-tolerant and drought-resistant crop varieties adapted to East African conditions.

**Estimated financing need**: USD 100–150 million for regional coordination mechanisms and shared infrastructure.

### 4.4 COP32 Positioning Recommendations

Ethiopia, as COP32 host, should champion the following priorities in the negotiation agenda:

1. **Adaptation Finance Scaling**: Call for doubling of international adaptation finance to meet the scale of vulnerability demonstrated in this analysis.

2. **Loss and Damage Framework**: Advocate for operationalization of the Loss and Damage Fund, with Sudan as a priority recipient given demonstrated climate-induced agricultural losses.

3. **Early Warning Systems**: Champion global investment in climate observation infrastructure, particularly in data-sparse regions like the Sahel.

4. **Climate-Smart Agriculture**: Position rain-fed agricultural resilience as a global priority, with Ethiopia as a demonstration model.

## 5. Limitations

### 5.1 Data Limitations

This analysis relies on satellite-derived NASA POWER data, which provides consistent spatial coverage but may differ from ground-based meteorological observations. Satellite products typically show higher temperature extremes and may underestimate precipitation in complex terrain (mountainous areas of Ethiopia and Kenya). The analysis would benefit from ground-truthing against national meteorological agency data.

### 5.2 Economic Impact Quantification

The analysis identifies climate signals and infers agricultural impacts based on established climate-agriculture relationships, but direct economic loss quantification requires supplementary data from national statistical agencies and FAO crop yield databases. The policy recommendations in Section 4 are grounded in demonstrated vulnerability but represent indicative rather than precise financing needs.

### 5.3 Temporal Scope

The 2015–2026 timeframe captures recent climate variability but may not fully represent longer-term trends or the full range of ENSO and Indian Ocean Dipole cycle effects. Extension of the analysis to 1991–2020 baseline (following WMO conventions) would strengthen trend attribution.

---

## 6. Conclusion

The evidence presented in this brief demonstrates that East African nations face differentiated but interconnected climate vulnerabilities requiring targeted adaptation responses. Sudan's extreme heat and drought exposure demands urgent priority attention in international climate finance allocation. Ethiopia's precipitation sensitivity, despite moderate temperatures, requires investment in rain-fed agricultural resilience to protect the livelihoods of millions of smallholder farmers.

The data supports Ethiopia's strategic positioning at COP32 as both a vulnerable party requiring adaptation support and a regional leader demonstrating climate-resilient development pathways. The five-country vulnerability gradient provides the empirical foundation for differentiated regional adaptation priorities, with clear implications for climate finance allocation, early warning system investment, and agricultural resilience programming.

The climate signal is clear. The impacts are documented. The policy response must match the scale of the challenge.
